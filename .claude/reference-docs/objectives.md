# Objectives

1. Informed Leadership: Leaders have up-to-date and trustworthy information about AI’s impact on society, learning, and the workplace so they can make better decisions. 
	- ***Success Looks Like*** 
		- President’s Council, faculty, and administrators are not relying on hype, anecdotes, or outdated assumptions about AI. 
		- There is a common baseline understanding of what is actually happening with AI on campus, in students’ lives, and in higher education generally. 
	- ***Examples*** 
		- Leaders understand what is fundamentally different now, compared to how we’ve used computers in the past; they understand key developments like alpha fold, Claude Cowork, and why those things matter.  
		- Leaders are familiar with core research studies (internal and external). 
2. Clear Governance: There is a clear, effective process for identifying questions and then determining how they will be answered, codified, and made available to the right audiences.
	- ***Success Looks Like*** 
		- It is clear who is working on what questions, when an answer is expected, and what level of authority is needed to approve them.
	- ***Examples*** 
		- Who can build what agents and what oversight should exist?  
		- Who should have access to copilot premium?  
3. Clear Rules: Employees and students understand the “rules of the game” regarding AI use. 
	- ***Success Looks Like***
		- AI use and innovation happens with confidence because people know when they are operating inside guardrails, in a gray area that warrants consultation, or clearly outside acceptable practice. 
	- ***Examples*** 
		- Faculty or admin who want to create an agent know when they need to contact IT vs. when they can act independently.  
		- Students understand what is considered cheating vs. not. 
4. Proactive Sharing: Lessons learned in one part of the organization are proactively shared with other parts of the institution
	- ***Success Looks Like***
		- Patterns, lessons learned, and promising practices are identified and shared in ways others can recognize and apply. 
		- The institution benefits from individual initiative without requiring central control of every effort. 
	- ***Examples*** 
		- Faculty lunches – 10 minute presentation on a success, 50 minutes discussion. 
5. Communication Hub: The various AI teams have effective channels to proactively share news, upcoming23 events, reports, etc. broadly. 
	- ***Success Looks Like***
		- Other AI teams have someone they can go to in order to “get the word out” about an event, a research report, a new development, etc., to their target audience.
	- ***Examples*** 
		- A digest highlights upcoming AI events, trainings, and forums from any other part of the AI strategy. 
		- The research team gets help to packaged findings into short, shareable summaries for campus use (E.g., short videos, briefings documents, etc.). 
		- Policy and guidance updates are announced with plain-language explanations and pointers to the official source.
